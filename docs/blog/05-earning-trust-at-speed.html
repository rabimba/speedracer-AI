Earning Trust at Speed

The model now generates tokens on the phone — fastest on the NPU. But that was never what carried the trust. This is the part that does, and it's been validated on a real recorded lap.

We've arrived at the question the whole series circles. Across four posts I've built a coach that reads the driver, splits its brain to beat the clock, and — by the end of Part 4 — finally runs Gemma on-device, with the NPU first to speak at 424 ms. Good. But a model that's fast is still a model that samples, and "the model felt like it" is not an acceptable answer when the message is "brake." So the reasonable question isn't "is the model fast?" It's: what carries the trust?

The answer is the part that was always meant to: the deterministic core. And it's been validated on a physical Pixel 10.

Trust is built on determinism

Here's the thesis the whole project rests on: you cannot trust what you cannot audit. A sampled language model is, by construction, not auditable in the sense that matters for safety — you can't read it and know why it said "brake." But the HOT path is hand-written rules. A senior engineer can read the DECISION_MATRIX and tell you exactly which condition fired a critical alert and why. That readability isn't a consolation prize for not using AI on the hot path — it's the entire point. The safety of the system lives in the lane a human can fully reason about.

And determinism is testable. So the validation is layered: vitest unit tests on the core logic (DriverModel, TimingGate, CornerPhaseDetector); a "Sonoma CSV" integration test that runs the whole coaching service against a known-good telemetry file; and a full on-device end-to-end suite that drives the native app on real hardware.

Until finally: a green run on real silicon

This is the artifact I'm most proud of. A recorded session on a Pixel 10 running Android 16 — a beginner Sonoma training scenario, played back through the native cockpit — produced 257 fused frames, 125 coaching decisions, and 126 audio events, and then passed every one of 16 independent validation checks. The two that matter most:

Five milliseconds. The reflex lane finished its job in a tenth of its own budget, and the spoken safety alert dispatched just as fast. That's the number the entire architecture exists to produce, and it held up on real silicon — not a workstation, a phone in a car-shaped test harness.

Transparency, all the way down

The other half of trust is that the system never hides uncertainty. If the model is unavailable, the app shows the deterministic fallback and says so. If the camera is still initializing, it says so. The diagnostics tab always offers the next actionable state instead of pretending everything is fine. And every native session persists the full fused-frame timeline and decision log — including audio dispatch latency evidence — so any lap can be replayed and audited after the fact. The validator I keep citing is reading exactly that artifact. The system grades its own homework, and shows its work.

And ever since: the part that isn't about racing

Strip the racing vocabulary away and look at the shape of what's left: a real-time system that fuses sensor data, makes an instant deterministic safety decision, defers slow reasoning to a model that's allowed to be late, adapts its output to the user's skill, and stays honest about its own confidence. That pattern isn't about cars. It's a blueprint for any high-stakes, real-time guidance problem — a surgeon's assistant, an industrial operator's aid, a pilot's prompt — where being right but late is the same as being wrong, and where trust has to be earned, not asserted.

That's why the split-brain, the queue, and the timing gate were built to be domain-agnostic from the start. Racecraft is the proof of concept; the trustable real-time coaching framework underneath is the actual idea.

I started this because I muted an app on a track day. The whole project is an answer to a simple question: what would it take to build the one I'd never mute?

Five milliseconds, an honest chart, and a green run on a real phone. That's the start of an answer.
